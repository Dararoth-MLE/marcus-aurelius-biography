In his own words, in translation

From the Meditations

Eight lines from Marcus's private notebook, translated by George Long in 1862. The numbers refer to the books of the Meditations, not page numbers.

Begin the morning by saying to thyself: I shall meet with the busy-body, the ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious, unsocial. All these things happen to them by reason of their ignorance of what is good and evil.
Meditations, Book 2
Whatever this is that I am, it is a little flesh and breath, and the ruling part.
Meditations, Book 2
Since it is possible that thou mayest depart from life this very moment, regulate every act and thought accordingly.
Meditations, Book 2
Remember that all is opinion.
Meditations, Book 2
The things which are external to my mind have no relation at all to my mind.
Meditations, Book 7
I can have that opinion about anything, which I ought to have. If I can, why am I disturbed?
Meditations, Book 7
In the morning when thou risest unwillingly, let this thought be present: I am rising to the work of a human being.
Meditations, Book 5
Always run to the short way; and the short way is the natural: accordingly say and do everything in conformity with the soundest reason.
Meditations, Book 4
