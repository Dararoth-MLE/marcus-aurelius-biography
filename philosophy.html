Four ideas from a working philosopher

Stoic Philosophy

Marcus did not write a philosophical system. He wrote reminders to himself. These four ideas recur most often.

Dichotomy of control

Some things are within your power: your judgments, your choices, your reactions. Everything else — other people's opinions, your reputation, the outcome of a battle — is not. Marcus returns to this distinction constantly, treating it as the root of most unnecessary suffering. People are disturbed not by events themselves, but by the opinions they form about them.

I can have that opinion about anything, which I ought to have. If I can, why am I disturbed? Meditations, Book 7
Virtue

For the Stoics, virtue is the only true good — not health, wealth, or reputation, all of which can be used well or badly. Marcus organizes his own conduct around four qualities inherited from earlier Greek philosophy: wisdom, justice, courage, and temperance. A life spent practicing these, he argues, cannot be made worse by anything outside it.

Memento mori

Marcus reminds himself, repeatedly, that he could die at any moment — not to despair, but to focus. If the present moment might be his last, there is no reason to waste it on resentment, distraction, or the pursuit of things that will not matter once it ends.

Since it is possible that thou mayest depart from life this very moment, regulate every act and thought accordingly. Meditations, Book 2
Duty

Marcus saw himself as one part of a larger whole — a citizen of the world before he was a citizen of Rome. He compares people who refuse to cooperate with one another to a hand that refuses to work with the rest of the body: a small, self-defeating rebellion against their own nature.

We are made for co-operation, like feet, like hands, like eyelids, like the rows of the upper and lower teeth. Meditations, Book 2
